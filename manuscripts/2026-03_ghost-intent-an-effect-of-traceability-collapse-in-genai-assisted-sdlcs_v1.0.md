## Abstract

As Generative AI (GenAI) shifts the human role in software development from creative authorship to review-centric oversight, the foundational assumptions of software traceability are undergoing a systemic failure. This paper defines **Ghost Intent**—the experiential manifestation of **Authorless Traceability Collapse (ATC)**—a structural condition where engineering rationale evaporates post-generation, leaving only behavioral remnants embedded in artifacts. We argue that Ghost Intent invalidates long-standing Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) assumptions and produces a **debug cost inversion**, where accelerated generation is decoupled from the non-linear expansion of diagnostic effort.

Beyond technical debt, this paper positions Ghost Intent as a **first-order governance and institutional risk**. By articulating its formal characteristics, observability signals, and multi-dimensional risk profile, we establish a critical conceptual vocabulary for AI-native SDLC formalization. This framework provides a basis for future regulatory compliance, decision-behavior governance, and the mitigation of "epistemic opacity" in AI-augmented organizational knowledge.

## 1. Introduction

Generative AI (GenAI) has fundamentally altered the mechanics of software development, shifting the human role from active authorship to review-centric oversight. While this transition appears to accelerate delivery, it introduces a new class of systemic instability in how software systems are understood, debugged, and governed over time.

Traditionally, software development relies on the assumption of **durable human authorship**, where the "why" behind a code change is preserved either in the developer’s mental model or through explicit traceability artifacts like commit messages and documentation. However, GenAI-assisted workflows frequently bypass this cognitive consolidation. Code is increasingly generated by stochastic models and integrated through rapid human validation, often without a stable underlying design rationale ever being formed.

Practitioners now report a recurring paradox: defects arise in code that was previously reviewed and verified as "correct," yet no diagnostic entry point exists to understand the specific intent or constraints that shaped its implementation. While version control systems remain intact and syntax is readable, the **decision context**—the justification for architectural boundaries and trade-offs—has evaporated. In these scenarios, debugging shifts from a deductive correction process to a form of **interpretive archaeology**, where engineers must reconstruct intent through behavioral inference rather than historical record.

This paper argues that these experiences are not merely anecdotal failures of individual skill, but reflections of a structural breakdown in foundational traceability assumptions. We define this phenomenon as **Ghost Intent**, the experiential manifestation of **Authorless Traceability Collapse (ATC)**. By formalizing these concepts, we aim to provide a vocabulary for a new class of **governance risk** where systems remain behaviorally functional but become epistemically opaque to their human stewards.

## 2. Background and Motivation

Traditional Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) models—ranging from Waterfall to Agile—implicitly rely on the assumption of **durable human authorship**. Traceability artifacts, including commit histories, pull request discussions, and technical documentation, operate on the premise that engineering intent is either explicitly encoded in these artifacts or retained within the persistent mental model of a human developer. In this conventional framework, "traceability" is the ability to link a high-level requirement to a specific block of code through a documented chain of human decisions.

GenAI-assisted workflows fundamentally violate these foundational assumptions in several critical ways:

* **Displacement of Mental Models**: Code is often generated through iterative prompting without a human engineer ever forming a cohesive, end-to-end architectural understanding of the solution.
* **Review-Centric Validation Gaps**: While human review validates behavioral correctness (e.g., "Does the code run?") at a specific point in time, it rarely captures or preserves the underlying logic, discarded alternatives, or specific constraints that shaped the AI's output.
* **Epistemic Opacity**: Organizations are increasingly accumulating "black-box" systems that are functionally operational but intellectually inaccessible. This creates a state where the system's behavior is observable, yet its design rationale is evaporated.

The motivation for this research stems from the emerging **"Knowledge Debt"** within AI-augmented organizations. As the gap between preserved artifacts and evaporated intent widens, traditional debugging and maintenance mechanisms become ineffective. This necessitates a conceptual reframing—moving beyond simple version control toward a model that accounts for the **Authorless Traceability Collapse (ATC)** inherent in stochastic generation.

## 3. Theoretical Framework: Ghost Intent and Decision Provenance

### 3.1 Defining Ghost Intent

**Ghost Intent** is defined as a condition in which software artifacts remain operational and behaviorally coherent, yet the engineering decisions that justified their structure, boundaries, or behavior are no longer recoverable. Unlike traditional undocumented or legacy code—which typically suffer from post-hoc knowledge decay—Ghost Intent arises from **absent-from-inception** decision capture.

It emerges when AI-assisted generation produces artifacts without ever forming a durable association between the code and the decision units, constraints, or alternatives that shaped them. Consequently, Ghost Intent is not a mere documentation gap but a **structural decoupling between behavioral correctness and decision provenance**, which manifests as a critical risk during system evolution or debugging.

### 3.2 Formal Representation and the Inference Gap

Ghost Intent can be minimally formalized as a discrepancy between preserved artifacts and absent decision records. Let:

* $D\left(a\right)$  denote the set of engineering decisions originally associated with artifact  $a$  (including constraints, boundary choices, and confidence assumptions).
* $A\left(a\right)$  denote the observable artifacts produced (code, tests, and configurations).

Ghost Intent arises when the set of lost intent  $I_{lost}\left(a\right)$  is non-empty:

$$
I_{lost}\left(a\right)=D\left(a\right)-A\left(a\right)\ne \emptyset
$$

In traditional SDLCs, human teams infer  $D\left(a\right)$  from  $A\left(a\right)$  through the continuity of authorship. However, under conditions of Authorless Traceability Collapse (ATC), this inference becomes **non-injective**: multiple, potentially incompatible decision paths can map to identical artifacts. This creates a **lossy decompression of engineering intent**, where stochastic generation introduces irreversible decision entropy into the codebase, rendering reverse reconstruction epistemically unreliable.

### 3.3 The Role of Intent Anchors

The persistence of engineering rationale depends on the presence of **Intent Anchors**—stable, navigable references that bind a design decision to a specific location within a system.

* **Implicit Anchors**: In traditional development, these emerge through authorship, architectural conventions, or long-lived discussion artifacts.
* **The Anchor-less State**: In GenAI-assisted pipelines, these anchors frequently fail to materialize, as code is reviewed and merged without persistent linkage to prompt context or discarded alternatives.

The absence of these anchors marks a transition to an **anchor-less state**, where artifacts persist as "behavioral ghosts" without recoverable decision coordinates. This structural vacancy is what fundamentally differentiates Ghost Intent from routine technical debt.

## 4. The Mechanics of Authorless Traceability Collapse (ATC)

### 4.1 Structural vs. Procedural Failure

**Authorless Traceability Collapse (ATC)** describes a systemic failure where traditional traceability mechanisms remain operationally active but intellectually void. While conventional tools successfully record *what* was changed and *who* approved it, they implicitly rely on the existence of an accountable human author to explain the *why*. In GenAI-assisted environments, this authorship is displaced, causing traceability artifacts to lose their explanatory power. ATC is therefore **structural**, not procedural: the tools function as designed, but their foundational assumptions of human intent persistence no longer hold.

---

### 4.2 Discontinuity in SDLC Assumptions

Traditional SDLC models assume that intent is durable, attributable, and recoverable across all lifecycle stages. In GenAI-native development, the speed of generation frequently outpaces human cognitive processing. This leads to a **discontinuous mapping** where:

* Code is produced without stable, formalized requirements.
* Artifacts are merged without a durable rationale for their internal logic.
* Systems are modified without awareness of the original constraints that governed their creation.

---

## 5. Observability and Manifestations of Ghost Intent

Since Ghost Intent is defined by the _absence_ of decision evidence, it cannot be logged through direct inspection; it must be identified through indirect systemic signals.

### 5.1 Indirect Observability Signals

The presence of Ghost Intent is marked by specific operational frictions:

* **Elevated Debug-to-Generation Ratios**: A widening gap between the speed of delivery and the effort required for diagnostic intervention.
* **Absence of Diagnostic Entry Points**: The inability of engineers to find a logical starting point for root-cause analysis despite having access to the code.
* **Contextless Semantic Search**: Search results that reveal behavioral patterns (the "what") but offer no explanatory design context (the "why").
* **Asymmetric Regression Risk**: Minor modifications resulting in unexpected system-wide failures due to invisible couplings embedded in generated code.

### 5.2 The Debug Cost Inversion

A critical consequence of ATC is the **Debug Cost Inversion**. As generation effort decreases through AI assistance, the effort required for comprehension and diagnosis expands non-linearly. Engineers transition from deductive correction to **interpretive archaeology**, where they must inductively infer the logic of an authorless system.

## 6. Risk Classification and Governance Implications

### 6.1 Multi-Dimensional Risk Profile

The following table classifies the risks associated with Ghost Intent across organizational and technical dimensions:

| Dimension                | Risk Manifestation                                                                            |
| ------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Artifact Alignment**   | Intent–artifact misalignment, version drift, and orphaned intent.                             |
| **SDLC Lifecycle**       | Generation ambiguity, review-phase decision loss, and maintenance entry collapse.             |
| **Amplification**        | Scale-based, iteration-based, and time-deferred risk amplification.                           |
| **Impact Scope**         | Degradation of maintainability, productivity illusions, and loss of organizational knowledge. |
| **Cognitive & Decision** | Accountability fog, cognitive entry-point collapse, and decision vacancy.                     |

### 6.2 From Code-Centric to Decision-Centric Governance

Ghost Intent reveals a significant **governance blind spot**: a system may be behaviorally correct and pass all automated tests while becoming progressively incomprehensible to its human stewards. This shifts the focus of failure analysis from code-centric defects to **decision-centric risk**. To mitigate this, organizations must reorient their SDLC models toward **decision-preserving lifecycles** rather than mere artifact pipelines.

---

## 7. Conclusion

Ghost Intent represents a novel failure mode in the era of GenAI-assisted engineering: the persistence of system behavior in the absence of recoverable intent. This phenomenon does not necessarily slow the initial development velocity, but it invalidates the metrics and assumptions by which that speed has traditionally been governed. By establishing the conceptual foundations of Authorless Traceability Collapse, this paper provides a framework for future AI-native SDLC formalization and the governance of decision provenance in increasingly opaque software ecosystems.

## 8. Related Work

Existing research on Generative AI (GenAI) in software engineering has primarily focused on productivity gains, adoption patterns, and technical challenges, but has only tangentially addressed the epistemic and governance risks arising from traceability failures. This paper builds on these foundations by introducing Ghost Intent as a unifying concept for understanding decision provenance loss in AI-native SDLCs.

Empirical studies highlight a **productivity paradox** in GenAI-assisted development. For instance, Afroz et al. (2025) and Giray et al. (2025) document significant acceleration in code generation tasks, but also note increased verification overhead and maintenance costs. Similarly, Rahman (2026) examines the long-term survivability of AI-generated code in open-source repositories, finding that while initial integration is seamless, subsequent modifications often lead to unanticipated regressions due to opaque decision-making. Reports from industry sources, such as Stack Overflow's Developer Survey (2025), GitClear's AI Copilot Code Quality Report (2025), Harness's State of Software Delivery (2025), and METR's Measuring the Productivity Impact of AI-Assisted Software Development (2024), corroborate these findings, emphasizing a "debug cost inversion" where short-term efficiency masks long-term comprehension challenges.

On the traceability and explainability front, Shah et al. (2025) argue for explainability as a compliance requirement in regulated industries, particularly for AI-generated design artifacts, where the absence of rationale can lead to audit failures. Watson and Van Itallie (2025) extend this to the "transparency imperative" in GenAI codebases, identifying epistemic opacity as a barrier to institutional knowledge preservation. Tsai (2026) explores related phenomena in "From Inference Creep to Risk Acceleration Pipelines," framing inference drift as a precursor to broader governance drift in AI-assisted workflows.

Governance frameworks provide high-level guidance but lack specificity for SDLC-level risks. NIST's Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework: Generative Artificial Intelligence Profile (2025) emphasizes decision accountability and auditability, while OECD's Framework for the Classification of AI Systems (2025) classifies AI risks across lifecycle stages, including epistemic uncertainties. However, these frameworks focus on runtime and post-deployment oversight, with limited attention to development-stage traceability collapse.

This work bridges these gaps by formalizing Ghost Intent and Authorless Traceability Collapse (ATC) as structural conditions, shifting the discourse from tool-level fixes to decision-centric governance. It establishes a conceptual vocabulary that complements empirical productivity studies and governance guidelines, paving the way for AI-native SDLC models that prioritize intent preservation.
